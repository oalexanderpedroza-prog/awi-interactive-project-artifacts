A structured evaluation framework for understanding how products and digital environments shape human outcomes across behavioral, cognitive, emotional, social, and informational domains.

UX Research Behavioral Science Human-Computer Interaction Responsible Innovation Systems Thinking Conceptual Research
Products increasingly function as environments that shape patterns of human behavior, attention, emotional experience, social interaction, and knowledge exposure. Yet the frameworks used to design and evaluate these products rarely organize around these broader patterns of human functioning. Wellness Verified explores how interdisciplinary research can inform structured approaches for making human outcomes more visible, more systematically understood, and more deliberately considered in design and evaluation processes.
01 — Project Overview

What This Explores

Every designed environment shapes human functioning. A social media platform structures attention, curates social comparison, and determines knowledge exposure. A productivity tool shapes cognitive load, interaction rhythms, and perceived agency. A learning platform influences motivation, skill development, and self-efficacy. These effects are not incidental — they are structural consequences of design decisions that operate through identifiable behavioral mechanisms.

Wellness Verified is a conceptual evaluation framework exploring how interdisciplinary research — from behavioral science, cognitive psychology, environmental psychology, human-computer interaction, and public health — can be organized into structured lenses for examining how products and digital environments influence patterns of human functioning. The project investigates how design teams might make human outcomes more visible, how evaluation processes might incorporate behavioral science reasoning, and how innovation processes might more explicitly account for downstream effects on the people who use the systems they create.

The framework is positioned as a reasoning tool and conceptual infrastructure — not as a compliance system, a rating scheme, or a restriction on innovation. It explores how the gap between what interdisciplinary research shows about products' human effects and how those products are currently evaluated might be bridged through structured, collaborative, scientifically grounded approaches.

Areas of Inquiry

Behavioral Influence Assessment

How can design teams systematically identify the behavioral mechanisms through which their products shape user behavior — including habit formation, reinforcement dynamics, and choice architecture?

Attention & Cognitive Effects

How do products structure the allocation of attention, the management of cognitive load, and the quality of decision-making environments?

02 — Problem Space

The Evaluation Gap

Products are routinely evaluated along dimensions of usability, engagement, performance, and commercial viability. These are important criteria. However, the broader patterns of human functioning that products influence — attention quality, emotional regulation, relational experience, knowledge exposure, perceived agency — are rarely evaluated with equivalent rigor. This is not because the relevant knowledge does not exist. Behavioral science, cognitive psychology, environmental psychology, and public health have produced decades of research on how environments shape human functioning. The problem is structural: this knowledge is fragmented across disciplines, uses inconsistent terminology, and has not been organized into forms that are systematically applicable to product design and evaluation.

03 — Conceptual Framing

Theoretical Grounding

Wellness Verified draws on six intersecting theoretical traditions to conceptualize products as environments that shape human functioning through identifiable mechanisms.

Behavioral Economics & Choice Architecture +

Thaler and Sunstein (2008) established that the arrangement of options — defaults, framing, option presentation — reliably shapes behavioral outcomes. Every product embeds a choice architecture. The evaluation question is not whether products influence behavior, but whether the behavioral influence embedded in design decisions is made visible and deliberate.

Environmental Psychology +

Kaplan and Kaplan (1989) documented that environmental conditions influence attentional restoration, stress response, and cognitive functioning. Ulrich (1984) demonstrated that physical environmental features carry physiological consequences. Applied to product design, these traditions predict that the informational, temporal, and interaction structures of digital environments influence cognitive and emotional functioning through environmental mechanisms.

Habit Formation & Reinforcement Theory +

Wood and Neal (2007) established that habitual behavior is triggered by contextual cues rather than driven by conscious intention. Products that embed their use in daily behavioral sequences function as contextual cuing environments. Understanding which reinforcement structures are embedded in a product's design is a prerequisite for evaluating that product's effects on behavioral patterns.

Self-Determination Theory +

Deci and Ryan (2000) identified autonomy, competence, and relatedness as fundamental psychological needs. Products that support perceived autonomy, build competence, and facilitate genuine social connection are more likely to support adaptive human functioning. Products that undermine these needs — through opaque control, learned helplessness, or social comparison — may produce engagement without satisfaction.

Social Comparison Theory +

Festinger (1954) established that individuals evaluate their circumstances through comparison to others. Digital social environments create structural conditions for frequent upward comparison — curated self-presentation, visible engagement metrics, algorithmically amplified aspirational content. Whether a product's social architecture produces beneficial or detrimental comparison dynamics is an evaluable design question.

Social Epistemology +

Fricker (2007) identified epistemic injustice as a condition in which individuals' capacity for knowledge acquisition is systematically constrained. Products that mediate information access — search engines, recommendation systems, content platforms — shape epistemic conditions at population scale. Evaluating these products requires examining not just what information is delivered but how the conditions for knowledge acquisition are structured.

The evaluation lens operates bidirectionally. Products shape human outcomes, and human behavioral responses feed back into adaptive systems that modify future design conditions. Wellness Verified examines both the forward pathway (design → outcomes) and the feedback pathway (behavior → system adaptation → modified design).

05 — Evaluation Lens Structure

Multi-Dimensional Evaluation

Products influence human functioning through multiple pathways operating simultaneously. The Wellness Verified evaluation lens organizes these pathways along four analytical dimensions.

Distal Effects
Direct Pathways
Indirect Pathways
Short-Term Effects
Long-Term Effects
Intended Outcomes
Emergent Outcomes
System-Level Effects
Proximal vs. Distal Effects +

Proximal effects are immediate consequences of design decisions — how a notification structures attention in the moment, how a default shapes a behavioral choice. Distal effects are downstream consequences that manifest over time — how habitual notification response patterns influence sustained attentional capacity, how default-driven choices compound into behavioral trajectories.

Direct vs. Indirect Pathways +

Direct pathways connect design decisions to human outcomes through a single identifiable mechanism. Indirect pathways operate through mediating mechanisms — a social comparison feature may not directly produce emotional distress, but may influence self-evaluation, which influences mood, which influences behavioral patterns.

Short-Term vs. Long-Term Effects +

Short-term effects are observable within a single interaction session. Long-term effects manifest across repeated use — habitual behavior formation, cumulative emotional exposure, gradual shifts in knowledge environments. Evaluation frameworks that only capture short-term effects systematically miss the outcomes most consequential for human functioning.

Intended vs. Emergent Outcomes +

Intended outcomes are design goals — the experiences and behaviors a product is designed to produce. Emergent outcomes are consequences that arise from the interaction of design features with human behavioral and cognitive systems in ways not explicitly anticipated during development. Both are legitimate subjects of evaluation.

09 — Framework Components

Evaluation Architecture

The Wellness Verified evaluation framework consists of three integrated layers: domain identification, mechanism analysis, and pathway mapping.

Layer 1 — Domain Identification
Which domains of human functioning does this product or feature potentially influence? Using the ten-domain taxonomy, evaluation begins by identifying the relevant outcome domains for the specific product context.
Layer 2 — Mechanism Analysis
Through what behavioral mechanisms does influence occur? For each identified domain, the framework maps the specific design features and behavioral mechanisms that mediate the relationship between product design and human outcomes.
Layer 3 — Pathway Mapping
What are the proximal and distal effects? The framework traces the full influence pathway from design decision through mechanism to outcome — distinguishing short-term from long-term effects, direct from indirect pathways, and intended from emergent consequences.
10 — Applied Workflow Tools

Design Team Toolkit

The following tools are designed as conceptual supports for reasoning about human outcomes within existing design and evaluation workflows. They are structured reasoning aids — not compliance checklists.

Human Outcomes Mapping Canvas

A structured template for mapping a product's potential influence across the ten human outcome domains — identifying which domains are relevant, which mechanisms are active, and where evaluation gaps exist.

Mechanism Identification Worksheet

A guided analysis tool for identifying which behavioral mechanisms (reinforcement structures, affordances, defaults, feedback loops, social comparison dynamics) are embedded in a product's design.

Loop Identification Tool

A structured protocol for identifying which behavioral loops are operating within a product environment — drawing on the Behavioral Loops Field Guide taxonomy of twelve loop archetypes.

Proximal vs. Distal Effects Map

A mapping tool for distinguishing immediate interaction effects from cumulative long-term outcomes — supporting evaluation that accounts for temporal dimension.

Human Outcomes Risk Scan

A structured scan identifying domains where product design may produce unintended negative effects on human functioning — framed as risk identification, not risk judgment.

Human Outcomes Opportunity Scan

A complementary tool identifying domains where product design may produce positive effects on human functioning that are not currently measured or communicated.

Mechanism Audit Tool

A systematic audit protocol for examining which behavioral mechanisms are active within a product environment and whether they align with stated design intent.

Design Decision Impact Map

A mapping tool connecting specific design decisions (interface layout, default settings, notification timing) to predicted human outcome effects across domains.

Feedback Loop Identification Guide

A structured guide for tracing how user behavioral data feeds back into system adaptation — making adaptive loops visible for evaluation.

Example Workflow Sequence

Step 1 — Identify interaction environment
Define the product, feature, or system being evaluated and its interaction context
Step 2 — Identify mechanisms present
Using the Mechanism Identification Worksheet, map which behavioral mechanisms are embedded in the design
Step 3 — Identify loop structures
Using the Loop Identification Tool, identify which behavioral loops are operating and their structural properties
Step 4 — Identify human outcome domains
Using the Human Outcomes Mapping Canvas, identify which domains of human functioning the product potentially influences
Step 5 — Examine proximal and distal effects
Using the Proximal vs. Distal Effects Map, trace both immediate and cumulative effects across identified domains
Step 6 — Examine feedback loops
Using the Feedback Loop Identification Guide, trace how user behavior feeds back into system adaptation
Step 7 — Identify open research questions
Document where evaluation reveals gaps in understanding that require further investigation
11 — Example Application

Illustrative Use Case

The following example demonstrates how the Wellness Verified framework could be applied to a hypothetical social media content recommendation feature. This is an exploratory illustration — not an evaluative claim about any specific platform.

Hypothetical example for illustrative purposes only.

Feature: Algorithmic Content Recommendation Feed

Step 1 — Interaction Environment +

A social media platform's primary content feed, algorithmically curated based on behavioral engagement signals. Users encounter a personalized sequence of content — posts, images, videos, advertisements — selected and ranked by recommendation systems optimized for engagement.

Step 2 — Mechanisms Identified +

Variable reward reinforcement (unpredictable content quality sustains checking behavior) · Algorithmic curation (content selection based on predicted engagement) · Social comparison affordances (visible engagement metrics, curated self-presentation) · Passive consumption structure (low-friction scrolling without reciprocal interaction) · Personalization feedback loop (behavioral signals modify future content selection)

Step 3 — Loop Structures +

Content Consumption Loop: content consumed → engagement signal → new content served → continued consumption. Variable Reward Loop: feed opened → unpredictable content quality → engagement maintained. Social Comparison Loop: social content observed → self-evaluation activated → continued observation. Information Refresh Loop: feed checked → new content may be present → checking reinforced on variable schedule.

Step 4 — Human Outcome Domains Potentially Influenced +

Attention patterns (fragmentation through variable reward and infinite scroll). Emotional experience (social comparison effects, mood-congruent content amplification). Social experience (passive consumption without reciprocal interaction; perceived relational quality). Knowledge exposure (algorithmically curated information environment). Perceived agency (opacity of recommendation mechanisms). Temporal experience (time perception during extended scrolling sessions). Behavioral patterns (habitual checking behavior).

Step 5 — Proximal and Distal Effects +

Proximal: immediate attention capture, momentary social comparison, content-driven mood shifts. Distal: habitual checking patterns established over weeks, cumulative social comparison effects on self-evaluation, progressive narrowing of information exposure through personalization, gradual shifts in perceived agency and autonomous control.

Step 6 — Feedback Loops +

User engagement behavior generates signals (dwell time, clicks, reactions, shares) → platform behavioral model updates → future content selection modified to maximize predicted engagement → user experience modified → new behavioral signals collected. This creates a co-adaptive system in which user and platform behavior evolve together over time.

Step 7 — Open Research Questions +

Does the variable reward structure of the feed produce measurable changes in sustained attention capacity over time? Do passive consumption patterns reduce perceived relational quality compared to active interaction patterns? Does personalization produce measurable epistemic narrowing across repeated use? How do different user populations (age, baseline mental health, usage patterns) respond differently to the same feed architecture?

12 — Synthesis

Emerging Themes

Products shape human functioning through identifiable mechanisms that are predictable from existing behavioral science — yet these mechanisms are rarely made visible in product evaluation. The knowledge exists; the evaluation infrastructure does not.

Human outcome effects operate across multiple domains simultaneously. A single design feature — an algorithmic recommendation system — may influence attention, emotional experience, social comparison, knowledge exposure, and perceived agency through distinct but interacting pathways. Single-domain evaluation systematically underrepresents product effects.

The most consequential effects of product design on human functioning are often cumulative and emergent rather than immediate and intended. Evaluation frameworks that capture only interaction-window outcomes miss the effects most relevant to long-term human functioning.

Making human outcomes visible does not require replacing existing evaluation criteria. It requires supplementing engagement, usability, and performance metrics with complementary evaluation lenses that surface additional dimensions of product impact.

Interdisciplinary collaboration is not merely desirable but structurally necessary. No single discipline provides an adequate account of products' multi-domain human effects. Effective evaluation requires integration across behavioral science, cognitive psychology, environmental psychology, social psychology, and information science.

13 — Key Insights

Research Insights

Framed as observed patterns across interdisciplinary literature and conceptual reasoning — not as validated empirical claims.
01
The evaluation gap is structural, not informational
The behavioral science knowledge relevant to understanding products' human effects already exists across multiple disciplines. The gap reflects the absence of evaluation infrastructure that translates this knowledge into forms applicable to product design and development processes.
Evaluation Infrastructure
02
Products are behavioral environments — and evaluation should account for this
Every designed product constitutes a behavioral environment with affordances, reinforcement structures, defaults, and feedback loops that shape behavioral patterns. Evaluating products only as tools that serve primary functions misses their function as environments that shape behavior broadly.
Behavioral Mechanisms
03
Human outcomes span multiple domains that interact — single-domain evaluation is insufficient
A product may simultaneously influence attention, emotional experience, social interaction, knowledge exposure, and perceived agency. Evaluating any single domain in isolation systematically underestimates the product's total influence on human functioning.
Multi-Domain Effects
04
Cumulative and emergent effects are more consequential than immediate effects
The most significant effects of products on human functioning are not the immediate interaction-level outcomes that current evaluation captures but the cumulative patterns that emerge over weeks, months, and years of repeated interaction.
Temporal Dynamics
05
Evaluation frameworks can support innovation rather than constrain it
Making human outcomes visible creates opportunities for differentiation, trust-building, and long-term engagement quality. Products that can demonstrate positive effects on human functioning have a credibility advantage that engagement metrics alone cannot provide.
Innovation Alignment
06
Interdisciplinary integration is structurally necessary for adequate evaluation
No single discipline — not UX research, not behavioral science, not public health — provides an adequate account of products' multi-domain human effects. Effective evaluation requires structured integration across fields, which in turn requires shared conceptual vocabulary and collaborative infrastructure.

14 — Implications for Research

Research Implications

Measurement Development

Validated instruments for assessing human functioning effects across the ten outcome domains in product interaction contexts — enabling empirical evaluation that complements existing engagement analytics.

Longitudinal Outcome Studies

Research designs that capture cumulative and emergent effects of product use on attention, emotional regulation, social experience, knowledge exposure, and perceived agency over extended time periods.

Cross-Domain Interaction Research

Studies examining how effects across different human outcome domains interact — whether attention effects amplify emotional effects, whether social comparison mediates knowledge exposure effects.

Evaluation Framework Validation

Empirical testing of whether structured evaluation approaches improve design teams' capacity to identify, understand, and address products' effects on human functioning.

15 — Implications for Practice

Design Practice Implications

Supplementary Evaluation Integration

Incorporating human outcome evaluation as a complementary layer alongside existing usability, engagement, and performance metrics — not replacing current evaluation but extending its scope.

Behavioral Science Literacy

Building behavioral science reasoning capacity within design teams — enabling designers and researchers to identify the mechanisms through which their design decisions influence human functioning.

Transparent Outcome Communication

Developing approaches for communicating products' human functioning effects to users, stakeholders, and governance bodies — supporting trust through transparency.

Agency-Preserving Design Principles

Favoring design approaches that maintain user awareness, preserve perceived autonomy, and support informed engagement — particularly in adaptive and personalized systems.

17 — Reflection

Scope & Limitations

This is a conceptual framework — not a validated evaluation instrument. The framework organizes interdisciplinary knowledge into structured lenses but has not been empirically tested for its capacity to improve evaluation outcomes.
The ten-domain taxonomy may not capture all relevant dimensions of human functioning. Additional domains may emerge as the framework is applied and refined.
Terminological variation across disciplines remains a persistent challenge. The framework addresses this by providing anchoring definitions but cannot fully resolve cross-disciplinary semantic differences.
The framework has not yet been tested with practitioners. Its practical utility, workflow integration, and organizational feasibility require validation in real design and evaluation contexts.
The example use case is hypothetical and illustrative. It does not constitute an evaluation of any specific platform or product.
The framework is designed as a reasoning support tool, not as a compliance system. Its value depends on adoption by teams committed to incorporating human outcome consideration into their work.
