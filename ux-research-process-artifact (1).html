About This Artifact

This research operating system reflects my personal approach to UX research, shaped by training in behavioral health and systems-oriented social science. It builds on standard UX methods while incorporating questions I often find important when evaluating products: not only whether an experience works, but what kinds of behavioral patterns and human outcomes it may support over time.

I recognize that this perspective is one of many valid lenses within UX research. My intention is not to replace established practices, but to make explicit the additional considerations that guide how I frame research questions and interpret findings.

Where This Perspective Comes From +

My approach to research has been shaped by training in behavioral health and systems-oriented social science, alongside developing practical UX research skills. Growing up in a first-generation immigrant family, serving as a Recon Marine, and working in behavioral health settings gave me early exposure to how powerfully environments, structures, and conditions shape how people think, adapt, and function over time. These experiences inform the questions I bring to UX research — particularly the question of what happens to people beyond the immediate interaction, and whether the patterns a product produces over repeated use are ones the team intended.

01 — What This Is

Research Philosophy

In my work, I try to integrate behavioral science reasoning into the standard UX research workflow — not to replace any of the core methods that make UX research effective, but to add a layer of inquiry that I often find useful alongside traditional evaluation.

Standard UX research asks: Does this work? Is it usable? Do people like it? The additional questions I often explore are: What behavioral patterns might this produce? What mechanisms could be driving those patterns? What might the downstream effects be on attention, cognition, emotion, social experience, and perceived agency? Are those effects intended or emergent?

The goal of this process is to support two layers of insight simultaneously: the traditional UX layer (usability, satisfaction, task performance) and the behavioral science layer (mechanisms, feedback loops, human outcome trajectories). Both layers can inform product decisions. Neither replaces the other.

Two Orientations, Working Together

Core UX Research Practices

Usability evaluation · Task performance · User satisfaction · Engagement patterns · Pain point identification · Feature prioritization

Additional Questions I Often Explore

What behavioral mechanisms might be at work? · How might cognitive load be distributed? · What feedback loops could form with repeated use? · What might the downstream effects be on attention, social experience, or perceived agency?
These are not competing approaches. The additional questions I explore build on and complement standard UX research methods. In many projects, the standard methods are exactly what's needed. The behavioral science lens is most useful when products influence habits, shape attention, affect emotional experience, or mediate social interaction over repeated use.
— How to Read This Page

This page documents how I currently structure my research thinking. It is not intended as a rigid sequence of steps. In practice, research moves iteratively between phases depending on product needs, timeline constraints, and organizational context.

The behavioral science components are optional lenses that may or may not be relevant depending on project scope. Many UX research questions can and should be answered efficiently using standard evaluative methods. This lens is most useful when products influence habits, shape attention patterns, affect emotional experience, mediate social interaction, or influence decision-making environments.

I share this framework to make my thinking transparent and to invite dialogue with other researchers and designers.

02 — End-to-End Process

Nine-Phase Research Cycle

The framework follows a nine-phase cycle. Phases 0–8 cover the complete research arc from infrastructure setup through knowledge capture. Each phase includes both standard UX research actions and behavioral science extensions — the extensions are there when relevant, and can be set aside when they're not.

Phase 0 — Research Operations Setup +

Purpose

Create infrastructure enabling continuous research across product iterations.

Standard Actions

Repository structure, tagging taxonomy, consent protocols, tool configuration, stakeholder mapping.

Behavioral Science Extension

I find it useful to define human outcome domains relevant to the product category early, so they're in mind throughout. This includes establishing behavioral mechanism tagging alongside standard UX tags, and creating clear AI usage boundaries — AI generates candidates, not conclusions. Defining what "verified finding" means up front (2+ evidence sources, counterexample documentation, traceability to raw data, human sign-off) helps keep synthesis honest later.

Key Deliverables

Repository structure, tagging taxonomy, AI verification protocol, decision log format, privacy/legal alignment.

Example Application

Notion AI Writing Assistant — understanding how AI writing assistance influences thinking clarity, confidence, and cognitive effort.

Scenario: Team wants to understand how AI writing assistance influences thinking clarity, confidence, and cognitive effort during writing tasks.
Human outcome domains relevant to this product category: attention quality, cognitive load, perceived competence, perceived authorship, learning effects, overreliance risk.
Mechanism hypotheses to track: AI autocomplete may reduce cognitive effort but also reduce memory encoding; suggestions may shape writing style convergence; rapid output may increase productivity but reduce reflective processing.
AI boundary: AI outputs treated as stimuli for observation, not as data sources. Human interpretation is the primary signal.

Phase 1 — Project Framing & Stakeholder Alignment +

Purpose

Convert a product team's question into a research-ready investigation.

Standard Actions

Review prior research and product roadmap. Run assumptions workshop with PM, design, engineering. Surface risk vs. uncertainty. Define research questions and success metrics.

Behavioral Science Extension

Alongside standard research questions, I try to frame mechanism questions: What behavioral patterns might this product feature produce? What reinforcement structures may be embedded in the design? What cognitive load characteristics does the interaction environment create? What feedback loops might form with repeated use? An "assumptions about human behavior" exercise — surfacing what the team assumes about how users will respond — often surfaces interesting gaps between intended and emergent effects.

Key Deliverables

Research brief, hypothesis statements, decision log entry, risk-uncertainty map, behavioral mechanism hypotheses.

Example Application

Notion AI Writing Assistant

Stakeholder question: "Does AI writing assistance improve productivity?"
Expanded framing: What behavioral patterns emerge when users rely on AI suggestions? What happens to perceived authorship? Does suggestion timing influence idea diversity?
Mechanism hypotheses: automation complacency, cognitive offloading, confidence calibration shifts, editing loop compression, idea fixation from early suggestions.
Research questions formed: How does AI suggestion timing influence perceived authorship? Do users critically evaluate suggestions or accept default phrasing? Does AI assistance reduce ideation diversity?

Phase 2 — Background Research +

Purpose

Build a grounded understanding of the problem space before designing the study.

Standard Actions

Review existing research, competitive analysis, analytics data, support tickets, prior findings.

Behavioral Science Extension

I find it useful to review relevant behavioral science literature — cognitive load, attention, habit formation, social comparison, feedback dynamics — as they relate to the product domain. Using AI to cluster sources by mechanism relevance can accelerate this, though I then manually validate those clusters. The goal is to map what is known, what is hypothesized, and what is genuinely unknown about how this type of product may influence human behavior.

Key Deliverables

Landscape synthesis document, literature clusters, mechanism-domain mapping, knowledge gap identification.

Example Application

Notion AI Writing Assistant

Relevant behavioral science constructs: cognitive load theory, automation bias, decision fatigue, effort justification, creativity constraint effects, choice architecture.
Known patterns from literature: people often accept default suggestions; early suggestions anchor later thinking; perceived fluency can be mistaken for quality.
Knowledge gaps identified: how AI affects reflective writing depth; longitudinal skill development effects.

Phase 3 — Study Design +

Purpose

Select defensible methods based on the research questions, product maturity, and cost of being wrong.

Standard Actions

Match questions to methods (interviews, usability testing, survey, diary study, analytics, A/B test). Define segments, sample plan, recruitment strategy. Set timeline and feasibility constraints.

Behavioral Science Extension

For each research question, I try to identify whether it requires behavioral observation, self-report, or both. Adding behavioral signal capture to the protocol — identifying what observable behaviors would indicate mechanism activation — helps ensure the study is designed to see what we're actually curious about. Where validated instruments exist (NASA-TLX for cognitive load, PANAS for affect), I try to incorporate them to support comparability across studies.

Key Deliverables

Research plan, sample plan, recruitment screener, method justification document, behavioral signal protocol.

Example Application

Notion AI Writing Assistant

Method selection: mixed methods — usability sessions observing writing behavior, think-aloud protocol, NASA-TLX cognitive load measure, follow-up reflection interview.
Behavioral signal capture plan: time to first edit, frequency of suggestion acceptance, revision depth, divergence from initial AI suggestions, self-reported ownership feeling.

Phase 4 — Instrument Design +

Purpose

Build the smallest, clearest set of research materials needed to generate the right signal.

Standard Actions

Draft discussion guide, usability tasks, survey instruments. Define success criteria and metrics. Pilot test with internal participants. Revise for clarity, fatigue, and timing.

Behavioral Science Extension

I try to add mechanism-probing questions to the discussion guide — questions that surface not just what happened but what behavioral or cognitive dynamic might have driven it. Debrief questions targeting perceived cognitive load, perceived control, and emotional response can reveal dimensions that task completion data alone won't show. I also pilot specifically to check whether behavioral signals are actually observable within the protocol as designed.

Key Deliverables

Discussion guide, usability protocol, survey instrument, behavioral observation sheet, version-controlled final materials.

Example Application

Notion AI Writing Assistant

Mechanism-probing questions added: "How much of this text feels like your own thinking?" "When did you feel most mentally effortful?" "Did the AI suggestions change what you wanted to say?"
Task scenario: Participant writes a short analytical paragraph with AI assistance enabled.
Observation targets: hesitation before accepting suggestions, rapid acceptance patterns, editing behavior after suggestion insertion.

Phase 5 — Fieldwork +

Purpose

Capture real behavior in conditions that approximate real use.

Standard Actions

Conduct moderated or unmoderated sessions. Collect video, audio, screen recordings, task performance data, survey responses. Document behavioral observations in real time. Share interim updates with stakeholders.

Behavioral Science Extension

I try to capture behavioral signals beyond task performance — hesitation patterns, attention switching, spontaneous verbalization, time distortion indicators. Moments where participant behavior diverges from their self-report are often particularly interesting — these gaps may indicate mechanism-level dynamics that self-report alone can't capture. Environmental and contextual factors that surround the interaction are worth documenting too, not just the interface itself.

Key Deliverables

Session recordings, transcripts, observation notes, behavioral signal logs, survey data, interim findings.

Example Application

Notion AI Writing Assistant

Observed: Participant accepts early suggestions quickly but later expresses uncertainty about argument ownership. Reports feeling faster but less confident in originality.
Behavioral signals: rapid interaction flow, reduced pause duration, increased editing after completion rather than during drafting.

Phase 6 — Synthesis +

Purpose

Move from raw observations to themes, possible mechanisms, and evidence-backed product implications.

Standard Actions

Affinity mapping, thematic clustering, pattern identification across participants. Identify pain points, opportunities, and severity. Write insight statements.

Behavioral Science Extension

For each theme, I find it useful to ask: what behavioral mechanism might explain this pattern? This means going beyond "users struggled with X" to ask what conditions in the interaction environment may have contributed to that struggle. I try to distinguish between usability findings (this doesn't work) and behavioral findings (this may work but produce patterns worth surfacing to the product team). The AI verification protocol — AI generates candidate themes, researcher validates against raw data, counterexamples documented, traceability maintained — is the safeguard that keeps this layer honest.

Key Deliverables

Affinity map, insight statements with mechanism labels, evidence verification table, human outcome domain mapping, behavioral mechanism synthesis.

Example Application

Notion AI Writing Assistant

Pattern: high suggestion acceptance rate early in task.
Possible mechanisms: default bias, automation trust, reduced cognitive effort threshold, anchoring effect.
Traditional UX finding: the feature increases writing speed.
Behavioral layer: the feature may also influence perceived authorship and idea exploration breadth — both worth surfacing for the product team to consider.

Phase 7 — Strategy Impact +

Purpose

Translate insights into design direction, prioritization, and roadmap decisions.

Standard Actions

Prioritize findings by severity, frequency, and strategic value. Run workshop with PM, design, and engineering. Connect insights to roadmap. Mark hypotheses as validated, partial, or uncertain.

Behavioral Science Extension

I try to present findings in two layers: (1) traditional usability and satisfaction findings, and (2) behavioral mechanism and human outcome observations. For mechanism findings, I try to be clear about what was observed vs. what is a hypothesis about longer-term trajectory. The distinction between "this requires an immediate design response" and "this is worth tracking over time" is often more useful to product teams than framing everything as urgent. I also try to frame design principles that account for both interaction-level performance and downstream behavioral considerations.

Key Deliverables

Research readout, opportunity areas, design principles, prioritized recommendations, behavioral trajectory considerations.

Example Application

Notion AI Writing Assistant

Design considerations surfaced: show suggestion alternatives rather than a single default; allow delayed suggestion reveal; highlight degree of AI contribution; enable reflection prompts after drafting.
Design principle: support efficiency without reducing perceived agency.

Phase 8 — Knowledge Capture & Portfolio +

Purpose

Convert project work into reusable organizational knowledge and portfolio-ready evidence.

Standard Actions

Tag insights in the repository. Log open questions for future cycles. Document tradeoffs and pivots. Translate into case study narrative.

Behavioral Science Extension

I try to document which behavioral mechanisms were observed, which were hypothesized but not confirmed, and which were unexpected. A running product behavioral mechanism profile — capturing known mechanism-level dynamics across research cycles — can be a useful institutional resource. The case study narrative benefits from a dual-layer structure: the traditional UX story alongside the behavioral science interpretation, clearly labeled to show what was found vs. what remains a working hypothesis.

Key Deliverables

Repository updates, case study draft, decision audit trail, behavioral mechanism profile, next-cycle research backlog.

Example Application

Notion AI Writing Assistant

Knowledge captured: contexts where AI improved writing flow, contexts where AI may have reduced idea diversity, conditions that preserved sense of authorship.
Open question for future cycle: does long-term AI usage influence writing skill development?

03 — Method Selection

Decision Framework

Method selection follows a decision logic based on question type, product maturity, uncertainty level, and cost of being wrong. The behavioral science extensions are layered on top of — not substituted for — standard method selection criteria.

🎙
Contextual Inquiry
When to use
Understanding real-world behavior in natural context.

What it captures
Environmental factors, behavioral patterns, workarounds, unspoken friction.

Behavioral Extension
Observe mechanism activation in situ — how environmental conditions may shape behavior beyond interface interaction.
🧪
Moderated Usability Testing
When to use
Evaluating interaction design with prototype or product.

What it captures
Task performance, error patterns, perceived difficulty, satisfaction.

Behavioral Extension
Capture behavioral signals (hesitation, attention switching, emotional response) that may indicate mechanism-level dynamics.
📋
Structured Survey
When to use
Measuring attitudes, preferences, or experiences at scale.

What it captures
Self-reported satisfaction, perceived usability, feature priorities.

Behavioral Extension
Add validated scales — NASA-TLX (cognitive load), PANAS (affect), perceived agency items, social comparison frequency.
📓
Diary Study
When to use
Understanding behavior and experience over time.

What it captures
Longitudinal patterns, habit formation, context effects, emotional trajectories.

Behavioral Extension
In many cases an ideal method for capturing mechanism effects that may only manifest over time — habit formation, feedback loop development, behavioral trajectory shifts.
📊
Behavioral Analytics
When to use
Understanding patterns of actual behavior at scale.

What it captures
Usage patterns, engagement metrics, feature adoption, drop-off points.

Behavioral Extension
Identify possible behavioral loop signatures — checking frequency patterns, session duration trends, feature return rates — that may indicate specific reinforcement dynamics.
🔬
Experimental Design (A/B)
When to use
Testing specific design decisions with controlled variation.

What it captures
Behavioral differences between conditions, statistical significance.

Behavioral Extension
Design conditions that isolate specific mechanisms — varying feedback timing, default structures, or social cue presence to test mechanism hypotheses.
04 — AI Integration

Responsible AI Usage

AI tools are integrated as accelerants, not substitutes. The protocol enforces a clear boundary: AI generates candidates, humans verify findings. I find this distinction important for maintaining research integrity, especially when working with qualitative data where pattern recognition can be shaped by expectation.

AI Verification Protocol

01
Force AI to output candidates, not conclusions
Prompt pattern: "List candidate themes + the evidence you would need to support each." AI is never the source of a finding — it is the source of a hypothesis to be checked.
02
Verification table required
Every candidate theme enters a verification table: Candidate finding | Supporting evidence (quote IDs) | Number of participants | Counterexamples | Confidence | Status (Unverified / Verified / Rejected).
03
2+ evidence rule
Nothing becomes a finding unless it has at least 2 verbatim quotes or observations AND appears across 2+ participants or sessions. No single-source findings.
04
Counterevidence mandatory
For every verified finding, at least one counterexample must be documented — or explicitly marked "none observed." This helps prevent confirmation bias in AI-assisted synthesis.
05
Traceability requirement
Every verified finding must link to raw data location — transcript timestamp, note ID, video clip. If you can't trace it back, it's not a finding.

AI Tool Stack

ChatGPT

Draft discussion guides, brainstorm hypotheses, generate candidate themes, structured outlines

NotebookLM

Source-grounded synthesis across uploaded documents, cross-document summaries, study-relevant questions

Perplexity

Web-backed scans for definitions, market context, prior art — rapid orientation with citations

Claude

Bias critique, logic stress-testing, long-form synthesis drafts, careful editing and rewriting

Gemini

Alternative framings, second-opinion drafts, risk and edge-case identification, missing-question suggestions

Whisper + Dovetail

Transcription → highlight → code → cluster → insight pipeline with traceability from raw data to finding

AI Prompt Log

Every AI interaction is documented: tool used, prompt given, input type, output summary, what was accepted/rejected. This creates an audit trail for research integrity and enables methodological improvement over time.

05 — The Behavioral Science Extension

Human Outcomes Integration

This is the distinctive layer of my approach. Alongside traditional UX evaluation, I try to surface how products may influence human functioning across identifiable domains through identifiable mechanisms. How relevant this layer is varies considerably by project and product type.

Domains I Often Consider

Domain 01
Attention
Is the product potentially fragmenting sustained attention? What notification, interruption, or content structures might be shaping attentional allocation?
Domain 02
Behavioral Patterns
What habits might the product be forming? What reinforcement structures could be producing those patterns? Are they aligned with user goals?
Domain 03
Cognitive Load
Is the interaction environment imposing excessive cognitive demands? Where might switching costs, decision friction, and information overload be occurring?
Domain 04
Emotional Experience
How might the product influence affective states? Could social comparison, feedback tone, or content exposure produce emotional effects beyond the immediate interaction?
Domain 05
Social Dynamics
Is the product potentially shaping social comparison, perceived norms, relational quality, or conditions for felt connection?
Domain 06
Perceived Agency
Does the user feel in autonomous control of their experience? Might opacity, personalization, or default structures be constraining perceived choice?

Mechanism Questions I Try to Carry Through Each Phase

Framing
What behavioral mechanisms might this product activate?
Background
What does the literature say about these mechanisms?
Design
How do we capture mechanism-level signals in the study?
Fieldwork
What behavioral signals are we observing beyond task performance?
Synthesis
What mechanism might explain each behavioral pattern?
Strategy
What are the downstream considerations — not just for the next sprint, but for long-term user experience?

09 — Operating Principles

Things I've Found Useful to Keep in Mind

01
Behavioral signals are often observable — you just have to design for them
Hesitation patterns, attention switching, time distortion, and emotional micro-responses can often be observed in standard research sessions. The difference is usually whether the protocol was designed to capture them. In many cases this requires only modest additions to a standard discussion guide.
Observation Design
02
Some UX methods may not always focus on longer-term behavioral effects
Usability testing is well-suited to capturing whether a product works. It may not always surface whether the product is forming habits, fragmenting attention, or producing social comparison over time. These dimensions often require a different lens — and a deliberate choice to look for them.
Evaluation Scope
03
AI is powerful for generation, requires care for conclusion
AI tools can accelerate hypothesis generation, theme clustering, and draft production. They also produce confident-sounding outputs that may not be grounded in evidence. The verification protocol — candidates not conclusions, 2+ evidence rule, counterexample requirement, traceability — is the safeguard I try to apply consistently.
AI Integration
04
Two layers of insight can give stakeholders richer decision material
Presenting both traditional UX findings and behavioral mechanism observations gives product teams more to work with. "Users struggled with navigation" is useful. "Users struggled in a way that may be related to attentional switching costs during multi-step tasks" opens a different kind of design conversation — not always necessary, but sometimes exactly what's needed.
Dual-Layer Analysis
05
Research infrastructure compounds — individual studies often don't
The repository structure, tagging taxonomy, mechanism profiles, and decision logs create institutional memory that makes each subsequent study more efficient and more grounded. Research operations are, in many cases, the difference between doing studies and building knowledge.

11 — Reflection

Scope & Honest Limitations

This operating system represents how I currently think about research. It continues to evolve as I gain more experience conducting research in product environments. I expect many elements to be refined through collaboration with designers, researchers, and product teams.
Not every project needs every phase or every extension. The behavioral science lens is most useful for products that influence habits, shape attention, affect emotional experience, or mediate social interaction. For many evaluative research questions, standard UX methods are exactly what's needed.
The AI verification protocol adds overhead that is justified for high-stakes research but may be unnecessarily rigorous for rapid-cycle evaluative studies. Practical judgment about when to apply it fully is part of the process.
The human outcomes layer is often conceptual — not all products have validated measurement instruments for every outcome domain. This framework attempts to identify what to consider; measurement development is an ongoing area of growth.
I'm early in my UX research career and continue refining this approach through coursework, projects, and collaboration with others in the field. I share this artifact to make my thinking transparent and invite dialogue.
