01 — Project Overview

What This Framework Does

Human wellbeing emerges from dynamic interaction between individuals and their environments. Across disciplines — behavioral science, ecological psychology, public health, systems theory, human factors — research consistently demonstrates that behavior and experience are shaped not only by internal characteristics, but by environmental conditions operating across multiple ecological levels simultaneously.

However, insights about environmental influence remain fragmented across fields with inconsistent terminology, incompatible conceptual structures, and limited cross-disciplinary synthesis. The Applied Ecological Wellness Framework (AEWF) addresses this by synthesizing interdisciplinary evidence into a coherent multi-layer ecological model describing how environmental influences interact dynamically across time to produce wellbeing trajectories.

Core Argument

Wellbeing is not primarily an individual trait. It is a system-level outcome shaped by proximal and distal environmental conditions operating across multiple ecological layers — conditions that are often invisible to those experiencing their effects, and underspecified in the design processes that create them.

Theoretical Foundations

Ecological Systems Theory Bronfenbrenner (1979) — nested environmental layers (micro, meso, exo, macro, chronosystem) and their bidirectional interactions with human development
Behavioral Economics Environmental defaults, choice architecture, reinforcement structures, and the systematic influence of contextual conditions on decision-making and behavior
Environmental Psychology Environment-behavior relationships, affordances, attention restoration theory, restorative environments, spatial cognition
Public Health Social determinants of health, upstream vs. downstream intervention logic, population-level environmental risk and protective factors
Systems Theory Feedback loops, emergent properties, leverage points, system dynamics, non-linear interaction effects across system levels
AEWF is a conceptual research framework. It is intended as an organizing structure for research reasoning — not a predictive model or intervention protocol.
02 — Problem Space

The Structural Gap

Research examining human wellbeing is distributed across multiple disciplines with inconsistent terminology and limited integrative conceptual structure. What ecology calls an "affordance" may be what behavioral economics calls a "default." What public health names a "social determinant" may be what human factors names an "environmental constraint." The same mechanisms get rediscovered across fields without recognition of convergence.

What Research Knows

Environmental conditions at multiple ecological layers systematically influence behavior, cognition, emotional experience, social connection, and physiological functioning. These effects accumulate across time through feedback loops and trajectory-level dynamics.

What Innovation Processes Do

Design and innovation processes typically optimize for usability, engagement, performance metrics, and conversion outcomes — while broader human outcomes (attention quality, relational depth, perceived agency, motivational orientation) remain implicit or unstructured.

Key Challenges

Terminological Fragmentation

The same behavioral mechanisms are named and operationalized differently across disciplines, preventing cross-field knowledge integration.

Missing Temporal Dimension

Design evaluations typically measure point-in-time outcomes, obscuring trajectory-level effects that accumulate through behavioral feedback loops across time.

Layer Underspecification

Innovation processes rarely distinguish between proximal influences (choice architecture, immediate affordances) and distal influences (institutional structures, cultural defaults) on behavior.

03 — Conceptual Framing

Framework Architecture

AEWF models human functioning as an emergent property of interaction between individuals and layered environmental systems. Rather than locating wellbeing outcomes in individual psychology, the framework positions them as downstream products of ecological conditions — where individual behavioral capacity is simultaneously an input to, and output of, the surrounding environment.

04 — Framework Structure

The Ecological Layer Model

The AEWF proposes six ecological layers adapted from Bronfenbrenner's bioecological model, extended to incorporate behavioral mechanisms, digital environments, and the chronosystem's temporal dynamics. Each layer contains environmental conditions that influence human functioning through identifiable behavioral mechanisms. Click any layer to expand.

Layer 0 Individual Behavioral Capacity Cognitive resources · Emotional regulation · Motivational orientation
Cognitive resources (attention, working memory, executive function) Emotional regulation capacity Behavioral repertoire and coping strategies Motivational orientation (SDT: autonomy, competence, relatedness)
Note — Layer 0 is bidirectionally coupled with all surrounding layers. Individual capacity is both shaped by and shapes environmental conditions at every level. It is not the origin of wellbeing but a mediating node within the system.

Layer 1 — Microsystem Immediate Behavioral Environment Built environment · Social context · Digital environment · Temporal structure
Built and spatial environments (physical affordances and constraints) Social environment (dyadic relationships, peer context, family system) Digital environment (platforms, algorithms, interaction interfaces) Temporal structure (routines, schedules, pacing)
Primary Mechanisms — Affordances, behavioral cues, choice architecture, reinforcement schedules, social norms, cognitive load. These are the environmental conditions most immediately present at each moment of behavior.

Layer 2 — Mesosystem Connections Between Microsystems Cross-context consistency · Norm transfer · Behavioral spillover
Home environment × work/school environment interaction Digital social environment × offline social environment Consistency or contradiction across environmental contexts
Primary Mechanisms — Norm transfer across contexts, identity signaling consistency, behavioral spillover effects, context-specific reinforcement patterns. This layer explains why changing one environmental context may produce unexpected effects in adjacent contexts.

Layer 3 — Exosystem Structural and Institutional Conditions Workplace design · Healthcare access · Community infrastructure · Platform governance
Workplace design and organizational policy Healthcare system access and quality Community infrastructure and resource availability Technology platform governance and design standards
Primary Mechanisms — Environmental constraints, institutional-level defaults, access barriers, distal affordances. Exosystem conditions often operate as background constraints that individuals experience as personal limitations rather than structural features.
+
Layer 4 — Macrosystem Cultural, Economic, and Policy Context Cultural norms · Economic structures · Policy environments · Media ecology
Cultural norms governing emotional expression and help-seeking Economic structures shaping resource access and time availability Policy environments governing behavioral default structures Media and information ecology
Primary Mechanisms — Identity-relevant constraints, cultural defaults, structural incentives, distal reinforcement patterns. Macrosystem influences shape what counts as normal, available, and possible across all lower layers simultaneously.
+
Layer 5 — Chronosystem Temporal Dimension Wellbeing trajectories · Cumulative effects · Life course dynamics
Wellbeing trajectories unfolding over time Cumulative effects of repeated behavioral patterns Life course transitions as leverage points Historical patterns of environmental exposure
Primary Mechanisms — Habit formation over time, cumulative cognitive and emotional effects, developmental timing interactions. The chronosystem explains why identical environmental conditions may produce different outcomes depending on when in the life course they occur and how long they persist.

05 — Research Questions

What We're Asking

Primary Research Question
How do multiple ecological layers influence patterns of behavior, experience, and human functioning across time?
SQ1 What environmental factors most consistently influence behavior and functioning across disciplines — and how are they named differently across behavioral science, public health, environmental psychology, and human factors?
SQ2 How do proximal environmental influences (immediate behavioral environment) interact with distal influences (institutional structures, cultural norms) to produce functioning outcomes?
SQ3 What behavioral mechanisms mediate the relationship between ecological conditions and wellbeing outcomes across the six proposed outcome domains?
SQ4 How do environmental influences accumulate across time through feedback loops and interaction effects — and at what points in the chronosystem is leverage most available?
SQ5 How do current innovation and design frameworks conceptualize environmental influence on human functioning — and where are the structural gaps relative to what interdisciplinary research documents?
SQ6 Where do disciplinary terminologies diverge for the same underlying mechanisms — and what conceptual alignment is possible across ecology, behavioral economics, psychology, and public health?
06 — Methodology

Research Approach

AEWF employs a conceptual synthesis methodology as its primary approach — systematic interdisciplinary literature review and construct mapping — with future empirical directions identified for each framework component. The current phase is framework construction, not empirical validation.

Future Empirical Methods

Qualitative — Expert & Participant Interviews +

In-depth interviews with individuals across diverse environmental contexts — to ground the abstract ecological model in lived experience and document how ecological layer conditions manifest in behavioral and experiential reports.

Expert interviews across ecology, behavioral economics, environmental psychology, and public health — to evaluate cross-disciplinary validity of framework constructs and identify mechanism categorization gaps.

Case-based analysis of individuals experiencing specific ecological conditions — to test the framework's descriptive adequacy against real biographical contexts.

Quantitative — Survey & Multilevel Modeling +

Cross-sectional survey measuring ecological conditions and wellbeing outcomes across a population sample. Instruments: Perceived Stress Scale, UCLA Loneliness Scale, Basic Psychological Needs Scale, environmental conditions items.

Multilevel modeling of population data — appropriately models hierarchical data structure (individuals nested in communities nested in institutions nested in policy environments) to estimate variance explained at each ecological level and test relative contributions of proximal vs. distal influences.

Evaluation — Framework Validity Review +

Expert panel review — framework validity evaluation by domain experts before empirical application. Produces expert feedback on construct validity, comprehensiveness, and practical applicability. Strengthens framework credibility for applied use and identifies revision priorities.

08 — Synthesis

Cross-Disciplinary Patterns

The following patterns emerged from cross-disciplinary literature synthesis and conceptual analysis. These are theoretical convergences — not empirically validated findings. Each represents a point where independent disciplinary literatures arrive at structurally similar conclusions under different names.

Behavioral science, environmental psychology, and public health consistently identify behavioral patterns, cognitive processes, emotional experience, social interaction, attention allocation, identity development, motivational structures, and physiological regulation as recurring domains of human functioning influenced by environmental conditions. These domains show convergence across disciplinary vocabularies despite terminological variation.

Environmental influences operate through a recurring set of mechanisms across disciplines: constraints, affordances, reinforcement structures, information exposure patterns, social signaling environments, and resource accessibility. The specific labels vary; the functional descriptions converge.

Effects of environmental conditions do not appear to be additive and linear. Instead, they accumulate across time through feedback loops and interaction effects that produce trajectory-level outcomes — wellbeing states that are partly determined by historical environmental exposure patterns, not only current conditions.

Design and innovation frameworks evaluated against the ecological model consistently address proximal and immediate behavioral outcomes (usability, engagement, task completion) while systematically underspecifying distal-layer environmental conditions and temporal trajectory effects.

The mesosystem layer — connections between microsystems — is particularly underrepresented in applied design thinking, despite substantial research on behavioral spillover effects, norm transfer across contexts, and the wellbeing consequences of consistency or contradiction between environmental contexts (e.g., digital vs. offline social environments).

Terminology fragmentation is not superficial. In some cases, what behavioral economics names "default architecture" and what public health names a "structural determinant" and what environmental psychology names a "contextual affordance" refer to the same underlying mechanism — producing parallel literatures that rarely cite each other.

09 — Key Insights

Tentative Patterns

These insights are directional propositions generated from conceptual synthesis. They require empirical testing and should not be treated as causal claims.

I-01
Environment-behavior relationships are multi-layered rather than linear
Human behavior is not adequately explained by single-level environmental variables. Outcomes emerge from simultaneous influence across microsystem, mesosystem, exosystem, and macrosystem layers — with interaction effects that produce non-linear dynamics not reducible to any individual layer.
Ecological Layers System Dynamics
I-02
Proximal environmental influences often mediate distal influences
Distal influences (cultural norms, institutional structures, economic conditions) appear to produce their effects on individual behavior largely through proximal mechanisms — shaping the choice architecture, affordances, and reinforcement structures that individuals encounter directly. Understanding this mediation structure is necessary for identifying effective leverage points.
Proximal vs. Distal Influences Behavioral Pathways
I-03
Behavioral mechanisms function as translation layers between environment and outcomes
Environmental conditions do not directly produce wellbeing outcomes. They operate through identifiable behavioral mechanisms — reinforcement structures, cognitive load effects, affordance provision, social norm signaling — that translate ecological conditions into patterns of action and experience. Mechanism identification is analytically prior to intervention design.
Behavioral Mechanisms Intervention Logic
I-04
Feedback loops produce trajectory effects rather than isolated outcomes
Repeated environmental exposure produces behavioral patterns that modify environmental conditions — creating self-reinforcing trajectories. Wellbeing outcomes are therefore partly determined by cumulative historical exposure, not only current conditions. Point-in-time measurement systematically underestimates these trajectory dynamics.
Feedback Loops Chronosystem
I-05
Terminology fragmentation obscures conceptual overlap across disciplines
The same behavioral mechanisms are named, operationalized, and studied independently across behavioral science, public health, environmental psychology, and systems theory. This fragmentation produces parallel knowledge bases that fail to accumulate — and limits the transferability of findings across disciplinary contexts.
Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis Terminological Alignment
I-06
Innovation frameworks often under-specify environmental effects on human functioning
Design frameworks that organize around usability, engagement, and task completion leave most of the ecological layer structure unaddressed. This is not incidental — it reflects a conceptual narrowing that systematically excludes distal-layer conditions, temporal trajectory effects, and multi-domain wellbeing outcomes from design evaluation criteria.
Design Practice Gap Human Outcomes
10 — Design Implications

Framework as Reasoning Tool

AEWF is intended as a reasoning tool — a structured conceptual scaffold for thinking about environment-behavior relationships — rather than a predictive model or prescriptive intervention framework. Its primary value is in how it changes the questions practitioners and researchers ask.

Potential Applications

Structuring UX research questions ecologically +

The ecological layer model provides a structured vocabulary for asking where in the system a behavioral pattern originates — and what layer-specific mechanisms are producing or maintaining it. Rather than asking "why do users behave this way?", AEWF-informed research asks "what proximal and distal environmental conditions are producing this behavioral pattern, through which mechanisms, and with what trajectory-level consequences?"

Mapping product environments to the ecological model +

Digital products and platforms operate across multiple ecological layers simultaneously. A social media platform exists at the microsystem (interface affordances, algorithmic content), contributes to mesosystem dynamics (digital-offline social interaction effects), and at scale shapes macrosystem cultural norms. The AEWF enables researchers and designers to map where a product operates in the ecological system and what layer-specific consequences follow.

Identifying unintended behavioral and wellbeing outcomes +

Design decisions that optimize for proximal-layer outcomes (engagement, time-on-platform, task completion) may produce unintended effects at other ecological layers — including distal reinforcement of attention fragmentation, erosion of motivational orientation, or mesosystem disruption of offline social environments. The AEWF provides a systematic structure for anticipating these cross-layer interaction effects before deployment.

Supporting interdisciplinary collaboration +

The AEWF's cross-disciplinary construct vocabulary provides a shared language for researchers from behavioral science, public health, engineering, and design to work on environment-behavior problems without being separated by terminological differences. By mapping how each discipline describes the same mechanism, the framework enables genuine knowledge transfer across fields.

Evaluating product ecosystems against wellbeing domains +

Rather than evaluating a product against narrow usability or engagement metrics, AEWF suggests evaluating product ecosystems against the six wellbeing outcome domains (behavioral patterns, cognitive functioning, emotional experience, social connection, physiological functioning, motivational orientation) — asking which domains are addressed, which are implicated but unmonitored, and which are adversely affected by current design decisions.

11 — Reflection

Limitations & Posture

AEWF is a conceptual organizing structure — not a validated theory. The following limitations are not incidental but structural. They define the current epistemic position of the framework and the work required to move it toward empirical adequacy.

Framework requires empirical testing. Proposed relationships between ecological layers, behavioral mechanisms, and wellbeing outcomes are conceptual propositions. Causal claims are not supported without empirical validation across diverse populations and contexts.
Terminology varies across disciplines. Despite the framework's attempt at cross-disciplinary synthesis, operational definitions remain contested within source disciplines. The current construct vocabulary reflects one synthesis approach among several possible.
Model complexity may require simplification for applied use. The full multi-layer framework may exceed practical working complexity for design and UX research contexts. Applied versions may require selective engagement with specific layers or mechanisms, with simplification trade-offs to be documented.
Causal relationships are not empirically validated. The framework proposes directional influence pathways. Whether these pathways hold across contexts, in what directions, with what effect sizes, and with what moderating variables is an open empirical question at every node.
Scope may evolve with additional synthesis. The current framework reflects synthesis of a defined literature set. Integrating additional fields (e.g., developmental neuroscience, anthropology, political economy) may require structural revision of ecological layer boundaries and mechanism typology.
Framework is intended as organizing structure, not prediction. AEWF does not predict specific behavioral or wellbeing outcomes from specified environmental inputs. It provides a structured vocabulary for organizing research questions, not a model for generating empirical predictions without further specification.

Research Posture

AEWF is offered as an exercise in interdisciplinary synthesis — an attempt to build a conceptual bridge between what research reveals about environmental influence on human functioning and how that knowledge is (or is not) available to the people who design the environments others inhabit. It does not claim to have resolved the theoretical tensions between source disciplines, or to have identified a definitive mechanism taxonomy. It claims to have mapped the terrain more completely than any single discipline has, and to have made that map useful for asking better research questions.

12 — Next Steps

Future Research Directions

Operationalizing Framework Constructs

Develop measurement approaches for each ecological layer's environmental conditions and each wellbeing outcome domain — moving from conceptual constructs to measurable variables with validated instruments.

Testing Ecological Layer Interactions

Empirical investigation of how conditions at one ecological layer constrain or enable conditions at adjacent layers — with particular focus on mesosystem dynamics, which are underrepresented in existing research.

Mapping Product Environments

Apply the ecological model to specific product ecosystems — mapping where each product operates across layers, what mechanisms are activated at each layer, and what cross-layer interaction effects are produced.

Examining Interaction Effects Between Layers

Investigate non-additive interaction effects between ecological layer conditions — particularly where distal conditions amplify or suppress proximal mechanism effects in ways not predictable from layer-specific analysis.

Integrating Framework into UX Research Workflows

Develop practical methods for incorporating ecological layer reasoning into UX research practice — including research question framing tools, environmental audit frameworks, and wellbeing-domain evaluation criteria.

Testing Framework Across Domains

Evaluate the framework's descriptive adequacy across distinct domains — healthcare, education, workplace design, digital platforms, community infrastructure — to identify where layer structure generalizes and where domain-specific modification is required.
