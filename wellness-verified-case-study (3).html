Products function as behavioral environments that shape attention, behavior, emotional experience, social interaction, and knowledge exposure through identifiable mechanisms. Yet the innovation ecosystems that produce these environments are rarely organized around human functioning outcomes. Wellness Verified applies strengths-based behavioral change principles at the systems level — using recognition, incentive alignment, and collaborative development to increase the prevalence of designed environments that support human flourishing. The intervention target is not the individual user, but the conditions shaping the conditions that shape user behavior.
01 — Project Overview

What This Explores

Every designed environment shapes human functioning. A social media platform structures attention and curates social comparison. A productivity tool shapes cognitive load and perceived agency. A learning platform influences motivation and self-efficacy. These effects are structural consequences of design decisions — predictable from existing behavioral science, yet rarely evaluated with the same rigor applied to usability, engagement, or commercial performance.

Wellness Verified begins from this observation but asks a different question than most responsible innovation frameworks. Rather than asking how products can be restricted from producing negative effects, it asks: how can innovation ecosystems be restructured so that environments supporting human functioning are more likely to be built, recognized, valued, and adopted?

This reframes the problem as a systems-level behavioral change challenge. The intervention target is not the individual user's behavior, nor even the individual product's design — it is the incentive structures, market signals, professional norms, and feedback loops that shape how innovation ecosystems operate. Wellness Verified applies the same behavioral science principles it studies — reinforcement, feedback loops, identity signaling, choice architecture, norm formation — to change the behavior of the systems that produce behavioral environments.

The Recursive Insight

Wellness Verified uses behavioral science to change the behavior of the systems that produce behavioral environments. The same mechanisms that shape individual behavior — reinforcement structures, feedback loops, identity signaling, social norms, choice architecture — operate at the systems level, shaping how organizations design, evaluate, and govern the products that millions of people interact with daily. By intervening at the incentive and norm-formation layer, WV creates conditions in which human-supportive design becomes the path of least resistance — not through restriction, but through recognition.

02 — Problem Space

Two Gaps, Not One

The conventional framing identifies a single gap: products influence human functioning, but evaluation frameworks don't capture these effects. Wellness Verified identifies a second, deeper gap: even where evaluation frameworks exist, the innovation ecosystems that produce products are not organized around human functioning outcomes. The problem is not only that products aren't evaluated for their human effects — it's that the incentive structures governing innovation don't reward human-supportive design.

Gap 1 — Evaluation
Products influence attention, behavior, emotional experience, social interaction, and knowledge exposure through identifiable mechanisms. Dominant evaluation frameworks (usability, engagement, retention) do not capture these effects.
Gap 2 — Incentives
Even where the knowledge exists, the innovation ecosystem's incentive structures — market signals, professional norms, organizational metrics, funding criteria — do not systematically reward design that supports human functioning.

03 — Conceptual Framing

Theoretical Grounding

Wellness Verified draws on eight intersecting theoretical traditions — spanning behavioral science, social work, economics, and innovation research — to ground its systems-level intervention model.

Strengths-Based Practice Theory +

Originating in social work (Saleebey, 2006), strengths-based approaches focus on identifying and amplifying existing capacities rather than diagnosing and remediating deficits. Applied to innovation systems, this means WV does not ask "what is this product doing wrong?" but "where is this product already supporting human functioning, and how can that be recognized, strengthened, and expanded?" Strengths-based approaches are associated with greater engagement, less defensiveness, more creativity, more voluntary adoption, and greater diffusion through peer influence — precisely the dynamics needed to shift innovation ecosystems.

Behavioral Economics & Incentive Design +

Thaler and Sunstein (2008) established that behavior is shaped by the structure of choice environments. Applied at the systems level, WV treats the innovation ecosystem itself as a choice environment — where the defaults, incentives, and feedback structures available to builders shape what kinds of products get designed. By introducing recognition, market signaling value, and reputational incentives for human-supportive design, WV modifies the choice architecture facing innovation teams.

Innovation Diffusion Theory +

Rogers (2003) documented that innovations spread through social systems via observable advantage, compatibility with existing values, trialability, and social influence. WV is designed to activate these diffusion mechanisms for human-supportive design: certification creates observable advantage (market differentiation), the framework is compatible with existing design workflows, the developmental pathway allows incremental adoption, and public recognition creates social influence through peer visibility.

Market Signaling Theory +

Spence (1973) established that in markets with information asymmetry, credible signals can resolve uncertainty and create value. Consumers cannot directly observe whether a product supports human functioning — this is a classic information asymmetry. Third-party certification functions as a credible signal that resolves this asymmetry, creating a trust mechanism analogous to LEED for green buildings or Fair Trade for ethical sourcing.

Implementation Science +

Implementation science studies how evidence-based practices are adopted within organizations and systems. WV applies implementation science principles by designing its framework for organizational adoption — embedding evaluation into existing workflows, providing structured tools that reduce adoption friction, and creating feedback loops that make implementation outcomes visible. The developmental pathway (not binary pass/fail) reflects implementation science's emphasis on iterative improvement.

Systems Theory & Feedback Dynamics +

Meadows (2008) identified leverage points — places in complex systems where small interventions produce disproportionate effects. WV targets two of Meadows' highest-leverage intervention points: the rules of the system (what gets measured, evaluated, and rewarded) and the goals of the system (what innovation teams are optimizing for). By making human outcomes visible as design criteria and rewarding their achievement, WV shifts the feedback dynamics of the innovation ecosystem.

Environmental Psychology & Behavioral Mechanisms +

Kaplan and Kaplan (1989), Ulrich (1984), and Sweller (1988) established that environmental conditions influence attentional restoration, stress response, cognitive load, and emotional functioning through identifiable mechanisms. These mechanisms — affordances, reinforcement structures, defaults, feedback loops, choice architecture — provide the evaluative vocabulary WV uses to assess how products influence human functioning. The mechanisms are the same whether applied to understanding individual behavior or to understanding how innovation ecosystems shape design decisions.

Self-Determination Theory +

Deci and Ryan (2000) identified autonomy, competence, and relatedness as fundamental psychological needs. Applied to WV's systems model, this theory informs both the evaluation criteria (does this product support user autonomy, competence, and relatedness?) and the intervention design (does WV's engagement with builders support their autonomy, build their competence in human outcome reasoning, and create professional relatedness through a community of practice?).

05 — Human Outcome Domains

Domains of Human Functioning

The following taxonomy organizes the domains of human functioning that products influence. These domains serve dual functions within WV: they are both evaluation criteria (what we assess) and recognition categories (what we certify).

Domain 01 — Behavior Patterns
Observable patterns of habit, interaction frequency, and behavioral persistence shaped by product design.
Mechanisms: Habit formation · Reinforcement dynamics · Choice architecture · Environmental cuing
Domain 02 — Attention Patterns
Quality, duration, and fragmentation of cognitive focus as structured by product architecture.
Mechanisms: Notification design · Information sequencing · Cognitive switching costs · Attentional capture
Domain 03 — Cognitive Experience
Demands on working memory, decision quality, and information processing capacity.
Mechanisms: Cognitive load · Decision architecture · Perceived complexity · Information structure
Domain 04 — Emotional Experience
Affective states, emotional regulation, and felt quality of experience during and after interaction.
Mechanisms: Social comparison · Content exposure · Feedback tone · Autonomy support
Domain 05 — Social Experience
Patterns of connection, comparison, perceived norms, and relational quality in digitally mediated environments.
Mechanisms: Reciprocity affordances · Social feedback metrics · Passive vs. active interaction structures
Domain 06 — Knowledge Exposure
The informational environment produced by curation and architecture — what individuals encounter, know, and can evaluate.
Mechanisms: Algorithmic selection · Information diversity · Source quality · Epistemic contestability
Domain 07 — Motivation & Agency
Perceived autonomy, competence support, and alignment between product use and self-directed goals.
Mechanisms: Default structures · Transparency · Personalization opacity · Goal alignment
Domain 08 — Identity Processes
How products shape self-presentation, identity reinforcement, role experimentation, and social signaling.
Mechanisms: Identity-relevant cues · Self-presentation affordances · Social role structures
Domain 09 — Temporal Experience
Subjective time perception, usage duration awareness, and temporal boundary clarity.
Mechanisms: Session boundary design · Time awareness features · Interaction pacing
Domain 10 — Environmental Exposure
Structural characteristics of the interaction environment — information density, stimulus frequency, choice complexity.
Mechanisms: Environmental defaults · Stimulus architecture · Choice structure design
06 — Developmental Pathway

From Evaluation to Development

Wellness Verified is not a binary pass/fail system. It is a developmental standards model — a structured pathway through which organizations progressively strengthen their products' contributions to human functioning. Evaluation functions as the beginning of a collaborative process, not the end of a judgmental one.

01

Exploratory Assessment

Initial signal of alignment potential. The organization engages with the WV framework to understand how its products relate to human outcome domains. No certification — this is orientation and self-assessment.

02

Foundational Alignment

The product demonstrates basic beneficial design characteristics across one or more human outcome domains. The organization has engaged with the evaluation framework and identified opportunities for strengthening.

03

Demonstrated Positive Contribution

The product shows clear, identifiable support for specific human outcome domains — grounded in mechanism-level analysis and consistent with behavioral science principles.

04

Strong Contribution

The product demonstrates meaningful and consistent beneficial effects across multiple human outcome domains, with evidence of deliberate design intent aligned with human functioning.

05

Exemplary Environment

The product represents a strong model for human-supportive design — demonstrating positive contributions across domains, deliberate mechanism-level design reasoning, and commitment to ongoing improvement.

The developmental pathway reflects implementation science principles: iterative improvement is more effective than binary compliance. Organizations at any stage receive structured guidance for strengthening their products' human outcome contributions.
07 — Evaluation Lens

Multi-Dimensional Analysis

The WV evaluation lens examines products along four analytical dimensions — proximal vs. distal effects, direct vs. indirect pathways, temporal scope, and intended vs. emergent outcomes.

System-Level Effects
Proximal vs. Distal +

Proximal effects are immediate consequences of design decisions — how a notification captures attention in the moment. Distal effects are downstream consequences that manifest over repeated interaction — how habitual notification response patterns reshape sustained attentional capacity over months.

Direct vs. Indirect +

Direct pathways connect design decisions to outcomes through single identifiable mechanisms. Indirect pathways operate through mediating processes — a social comparison feature may influence self-evaluation, which influences mood, which influences behavioral patterns.

Short-Term vs. Long-Term +

Short-term effects occur within a single session. Long-term effects emerge across weeks, months, and years of repeated use — habit formation, cumulative emotional exposure, gradual epistemic narrowing. The most consequential human functioning effects are typically long-term.

Intended vs. Emergent +

Intended outcomes are design goals. Emergent outcomes arise from the interaction of design features with human cognitive and behavioral systems in ways not anticipated during development. WV evaluates both — and treats emergent positive outcomes as opportunities for amplification.

08 — Comparative Positioning

How WV Relates to Existing Frameworks

Wellness Verified operates within the developmental standards tradition — joining frameworks like LEED, B Corp, and WELL Building Standard that create market incentives for beneficial practices rather than enforcing minimum compliance.

LEED (Green Building)

Parallel: Created a new market category for sustainable construction by making environmental impact visible, measurable, and commercially valuable. Buildings command 31% rent premiums with LEED certification. WV aims to create an equivalent signal for human-supportive design.

Difference: LEED evaluates physical environments. WV evaluates behavioral environments — including digital systems.

B Corp Certification

Parallel: Uses comprehensive standards to certify companies that balance profit with positive social impact. Creates identity signaling ("we are a responsible company") that drives voluntary adoption and cultural prestige. WV applies similar identity dynamics to human outcome design.

Difference: B Corp evaluates organizational practices broadly. WV evaluates specific products' effects on human functioning through mechanism-level analysis.

WELL Building Standard

Parallel: Evaluates built environments for their effects on human health and wellbeing — air quality, light, thermal comfort, movement, mind. The closest structural analog to WV in existing certification ecosystems.

Difference: WELL evaluates physical built environments. WV extends the same logic to digital and product environments.

Responsible AI / Ethical Tech

Parallel: Addresses concerns about technology's effects on people. Growing area of industry and policy attention.

Difference: Most responsible AI frameworks focus on fairness, bias, and transparency. WV focuses on behavioral, cognitive, emotional, social, and epistemic outcomes — a broader human functioning lens. And WV uses strengths-based incentives rather than risk-only frameworks.

13 — Example Application

Illustrative Use Case

The following example demonstrates how the Wellness Verified framework could be applied to a hypothetical learning platform feature. This is a strengths-focused illustration — identifying beneficial patterns and opportunities for amplification.

Hypothetical example for illustrative purposes only.

Feature: Adaptive Learning Platform with Progress Tracking

Step 1 — Interaction Environment +

An online learning platform with adaptive difficulty, progress tracking, spaced repetition, and peer discussion features. Users engage with structured learning modules, receive skill-based feedback, and participate in community discussion forums.

Step 2 — Strengths Identified +

Skill Development Loop actively operating (practice → competence feedback → self-efficacy → continued practice). Progress Feedback Loop provides clear advancement signals. Spaced repetition aligns with memory consolidation research. Adaptive difficulty supports flow state conditions. Community discussion supports social learning and belongingness.

Step 3 — Mechanisms Present +

Mastery motivation reinforcement (competence feedback strengthens practice behavior). Goal-gradient effect (progress indicators accelerate engagement as milestones approach). Social learning dynamics (peer discussion normalizes struggle and supports collaborative meaning-making). Autonomy support (learner controls pacing and topic selection within structured curriculum).

Step 4 — Human Outcome Domains Supported +

Cognitive experience: Adaptive difficulty manages cognitive load. Motivation and agency: Progress tracking supports self-efficacy and perceived competence. Social experience: Discussion forums support belonging and peer connection. Behavior patterns: Spaced repetition supports adaptive study habits. Identity processes: Skill mastery reinforces learner identity.

Step 5 — Proximal and Distal Effects +

Proximal: Immediate competence feedback, session-level progress signals, community interaction. Distal: Skill development over weeks and months, study habit formation, learner identity strengthening, professional confidence building.

Step 6 — Opportunities for Strengthening +

Attention domain: Could introduce session boundary awareness features to support sustainable study patterns. Temporal experience: Could add time awareness tools that help learners maintain healthy study rhythms. Emotional experience: Could strengthen emotional support during frustration moments rather than only rewarding success. Knowledge exposure: Could introduce diverse perspective features in discussion forums.

Step 7 — Developmental Pathway Position +

Current assessment: Stage 3 — Demonstrated Positive Contribution. The platform shows clear support for multiple human outcome domains (cognition, motivation, social experience) with identifiable mechanism-level design reasoning. Pathway to Stage 4: Strengthening attention and temporal experience domains, adding emotional support features, and demonstrating consistent positive effects across a broader range of user populations.

14 — Synthesis

Emerging Themes

The most effective way to change what products do to people is to change what gets recognized, valued, and rewarded in the innovation ecosystem. Intervening at the incentive layer is higher leverage than intervening at the product layer — because incentives shape the conditions that shape all products.

Strengths-based approaches produce qualitatively different innovation dynamics than risk-only frameworks. Recognition of beneficial design generates engagement, creativity, voluntary adoption, identity formation, and peer diffusion. Penalty for harmful design generates compliance, defensiveness, and minimum effort.

Human outcome evaluation does not require replacing existing product metrics. It requires supplementing engagement, usability, and performance measures with structured lenses that surface additional dimensions of product impact — making the full range of design consequences visible for deliberate consideration.

The developmental standards model — iterative improvement through collaborative guidance rather than binary pass/fail judgment — aligns with how organizations actually change. Implementation science consistently shows that sustained change requires ongoing support, visible progress, and social reinforcement.

The recursive structure of WV — using behavioral mechanisms to change the systems that produce behavioral environments — is itself a demonstration of the framework's core thesis: that behavioral science can inform not just the design of products but the design of the systems that design products.

15 — Key Insights

Research Insights

Framed as conceptual reasoning grounded in interdisciplinary research — not as validated empirical claims.
01
The intervention target is not user behavior — it is the conditions shaping design decisions
WV operates at the incentive layer of the innovation ecosystem. By changing what gets recognized, valued, and rewarded, it changes the behavioral environment facing builders — which changes what gets built, which changes what users experience. This is systems-level behavioral change.
Systems Intervention
02
Recognition creates a reinforcing loop that enforcement cannot produce
Strengths-based recognition generates engagement, identity formation ("we are a human-supportive builder"), reputational competition, and voluntary peer diffusion. These dynamics are structurally unavailable to enforcement-only frameworks, which produce compliance without cultural change.
Incentive Design
03
Products are behavioral environments — and evaluation should account for this
Every designed product constitutes a behavioral environment with affordances, reinforcement structures, defaults, and feedback loops. Evaluating products only as tools that serve primary functions misses their structural influence on human behavior, cognition, emotion, social experience, and knowledge.
Behavioral Mechanisms
04
Human outcome effects span multiple domains that interact — single-domain evaluation is insufficient
A product may simultaneously influence attention, emotional experience, social comparison, knowledge exposure, and perceived agency through interacting pathways. Evaluation that examines any single domain in isolation systematically underrepresents the product's total influence.
Multi-Domain Effects
05
Developmental pathways produce better outcomes than binary certification
Implementation science shows that organizations change through iterative improvement, not through compliance checkpoints. A developmental model — where any organization can enter, receive guidance, and progress — creates more sustained improvement than pass/fail systems.
Developmental Standards
06
Making human outcomes visible creates innovation opportunity, not constraint
Products that can demonstrate positive contributions to human functioning have a differentiation advantage, a trust advantage, and a long-term engagement quality advantage that engagement metrics alone cannot provide. Human outcome visibility is a competitive resource.

19 — Reflection

Scope & Limitations

This is a conceptual framework and systems intervention model — not a validated certification system. The approach is grounded in interdisciplinary theory but has not been empirically tested for its capacity to shift innovation ecosystem behavior.
The ten-domain taxonomy may not capture all relevant dimensions of human functioning. Additional domains may emerge as the framework is applied.
The systems-level behavioral change model assumes that recognition and incentive alignment can shift innovation ecosystem dynamics — an assumption that requires empirical validation.
The developmental pathway stages are conceptual and would require operationalization, reliability testing, and inter-rater agreement development before applied use.
The comparative positioning with LEED, B Corp, and WELL is structural — WV has not yet achieved the market penetration, institutional integration, or empirical track record of these established frameworks.
The framework has not yet been tested with practitioners. Its practical utility, workflow integration, and organizational adoption dynamics require validation.
